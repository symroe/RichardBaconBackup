It is time to make self-built homes a “mainstream part” of Britain's housing market


FOR PEOPLE who want their own home, it should be quite normal to find a plot of land and then build a house, or to get a builder to build one for you. And in many countries, it is indeed normal. In Canada, Germany, France, Ireland and Sweden, self-build and custom build accounts for over 50% of the national housing supply. In tiny Austria, it is 86%.

In the United Kingdom it is we who are the outliers, with self-build accounting for less than a tenth of the UK’s housing supply. Although there are many in the UK who would like to build their own homes or commission a builder to construct exactly what they want, the sector is still seen as an elite club only open to the highly eccentric or extremely affluent.

The result? As the presenter of Channel Four’s Grand Designs Kevin McCloud puts it: “We build some of the poorest performing, most expensive and smallest homes in Europe.”

The recent Budget provides hope for a real shift in attitudes and practice. The Chancellor has announced that £150 million will be available to help local authorities develop 10,000 “serviced plots” - fully serviced sites where the foundations, drainage and other utilities are pre-laid but the remainder is left as a blank canvass, leaving people to choose for themselves what they would like to see built. In the Netherlands, where they have done this, people have camped out through the night for the chance to buy a plot, in a way we only see in Britain for the January sales in Oxford Street. Nor is it only about home ownership.

Community building groups on the Continent have shown that this approach has considerable potential for the rented sector too.

With the right help and attitudes it can be made much easier - and empowering. Examples from Germany include a group of older women who wanted to live together in a block, knowing there would be plenty of friends nearby; and parents whose children have special needs who built a small community with its own special school. There is no good reason why self-build and custom build cannot become a mainstream part of the UK housing market. Many of the supposed problems are quite capable of being solved.

Some say: “Where would all the land come from?” The large volume housebuilders have already have very considerable landbanks - but the system encourages them to sit on these assets for decades until conditions are “right” to build and sell a house at a profit. This is perfectly rational behaviour within the confines of the current system, but it does not help the young couple in my rural constituency who desperately want to get their own house yet who cannot see how they will ever manage to do it. What if it were normal for large-scale developers to provide serviced plots to give their customers more choice? What if local planners also saw this as normal behaviour and encouraged it? Or even required it? The new national planning framework does impose obligations on local authorities to measure demand for self-build and to take steps to meet it - but few authorities have done this yet.

Some say this approach is not “scalable” and so could never make a serious difference to our housing crisis. Yet the failure of “targets” in the public sector has shown us that if one concentrates on removing obstacles rather than on obsessing about targets, it is possible to achieve performance improvements much greater than any target one might have dared to set. Ted Stevens of the National Self-Build Association has estimated that there are seven million people currently researching how to build their own homes.

Quite simply, if it were easier to do self and custom build, there would be more of it. Much more. It is perfectly possible to make self-build a mainstream part of our housing supply, as it already is in other countries. It is time we stopped treating self-build and custom build as an enviable dream for the lucky few and started to make it an affordable reality open to all.

Richard Bacon is MP for South Norfolk and Chairman of the All Party Parliamentary Group on Self Build, Custom Build and Independent Housebuilding

On Tuesday 25th March at 5.00pm in Committee Room 10, Kevin McCloud of Channel Four’s Grand Designs and Ted Stevens from the National Self-Build Association will address MPs and Peers at a meeting of the All Party Parliamentary Group on Self Build, Custom Build and Independent Housebuilding.
