BACON BITS: Richard's monthly Diss Express column

South Norfolk MP Richard Bacon brings Diss Express readers up to date with some of his recent work in the community.

HARLESTON NATIONWIDE
Nationwide Building Society’s decision to close its branch in Harleston has rightly attracted much dismay in the town.  Following on from the closure of HSBC, residents are determined not to let another financial institution close its doors locally without a fight. 

I recently met local business leaders who left me in no doubt about the strength of local feeling and Nationwide bosses need to understand just how important the branch is to Harleston. Together with local business leaders, I am hoping to meet Nationwide in the near future to ask them to review this decision.

I hope that Nationwide’s bosses can be persuaded to change their mind, but keeping the branch open will certainly be an uphill struggle.   In the meantime, if you’re a customer of the Harleston Nationwide, please do keep on using the branch.

PYLONS & THE WAVENEY
There is renewed concern about National Grid’s plans to connect cables from offshore windfarms to the electricity network, especially because one possible option is to put pylons along the Waveney Valley. 

Readers may recall that last year I met National Grid at Wortwell Community Centre together with hundreds of Waveney Valley residents, and representatives from local parish councils, to talk about such a possibility.  There was a strong and united front against the idea.  We left National Grid in no doubt that pylons have no place in the Waveney Valley and that any attempt to proceed would face the fiercest opposition.  

I am keeping a close eye on this issue and I will be pressing home the case for undergrounding any lines coming through Norfolk.

A GRAND OCCASION
I wanted to finish my column this month by congratulating the people of Diss and the wider area for the magnificent reception they gave to the First Battalion of the Royal Anglian Regiment earlier this month.

The crowd’s respect and affection for the Vikings was very clear, and the turnout - especially compared to much larger towns where the Royal Anglians have paraded, such as Ipswich and Peterborough - was quite exceptional.  It meant the occasion really felt like a family affair.  

At a civic reception afterwards, I chatted to members of the Regiment who were quite obviously overwhelmed by the warmth of their reception.

This was a grand occasion, at which I was honoured to be present.  Diss did the Regiment proud and my heartiest thanks go out to everyone involved.

30 November 2012
