Gordon's spending spree isn't doing public services any good

Ministers and civil servants are rewarded for spending - not making things work, says Richard Bacon.
  

Politics may be about power, but power is usually about money. And in politics the truth is that no amount of scheming and dreaming matters very much if you don't have the cash to put your plans into action.

So if Gordon Brown spends more on schools, or less on roads, it's not just teachers and students who get their hopes up, nor just motorists who despair. Ministers know their political futures depend on how much money they receive. Spend more and you are powerful. Spend less and you are weak.

This creates a big problem, though. For a rising minister, getting headlines by announcing an exciting new programme is much sexier than seeing through a difficult policy and delivering results. By the time people can see the results the minister will have moved on to something else.

It's no better for permanent officials. Sometimes they're moved even more often than ministers. How do you get ahead in Whitehall? Not by delivering programmes. No baby mandarin wants to spend years organising benefit payments or checking hospitals are cleaned properly. The smart jobs are in advising ministers and crafting policy.

Recently, one senior civil servant, addressing a group of Norfolk farmers, actually said: "My job is not to deliver programmes but to provide strategic input into a multiplicity of strategies." Any farmer working through the night getting in the sugar beet crop would be forgiven for blowing a large raspberry.

Why is it like this? Because the skill most valued in Whitehall is the ability to explain why something that looks like a disaster is in fact a triumph or, at least, more than one had any right to expect in the circumstances.

And why is that? Because politicians look to civil servants to help them out of tight spots - often created when they didn't pay enough attention in the first place.

A recent report to Parliament from the National Audit Office* says there is a risk that the extra cash going into public services will just get wasted. It points out that a mere one per cent improvement in how the money available is spent over the next three years would release some £14.5 billion for front-line services. That's more than 4p on income tax in one year.

The trouble is that the system rewards spending money, not looking after it.

There is a black hole at the heart of British government. If taxpayers knew just how big it was they would throw a fit. The public sector in Britain loses billions - by my calculation between £29 billion and £44 billion every year. That's £500 to £700 for every man, woman and child in the country.

Where does the money go? It's not just the familiar mantra about extra special advisers in Whitehall. True, central government administration costs have skyrocketed from £14.8 billion in 1997 to nearly £21 billion now. But it goes much deeper.

The Department for Work and Pensions has received a black mark from the auditors every year since 1988 because of the volume of fraud. Yet it doesn't even have a target date for producing a clean set of accounts. Each year, between £3 billion and £7 billion goes south through fraud and error. Five years ago, civil servants running social security were slammed by MPs for not knowing if housing benefit fraud was going up or down. They still don't.

There are 40 new Apache helicopters worth more than £1.2 billion stored in a warehouse on Salisbury Plain, at a cost of £6 million, because the Ministry of Defence didn't train enough pilots in time.

Customs & Excise loses between £7 billion and £10 billion on unpaid VAT and £7 billion on excise duties on alcohol, tobacco and fuel. The Inland Revenue says £2.5 billion is at risk annually because of income tax self-assessment.

Each year taxpayers spend £100 million training teachers who never set foot in a classroom. Just one failed computer scheme, the Benefit Payments Card, cost £1.1 billion before it was scrapped - and there are many more. A computer system for the probation service had seven managers in seven years, five of whom knew nothing about project management. The project ran 70 per cent over budget. A computer system for the magistrates' courts saw costs explode from £156 million to nearly £400 million. It doesn't work properly, of course. That £400 million would have paid for all the schools in Norfolk for a year. The new £500 million computer for the Child Support Agency (CSA) isn't working, either: that's the one which replaced the old CSA computer because it was inadequate.

The Government Communications Headquarters expected to pay £40 million for moving the signals intelligence computer systems into its new building. Managers told the GCHQ board it would cost £20 million. Actually, it cost £400 million.

The Government thinks the National Health Service loses 16 to 20 per cent of its budget each year through waste, mismanagement, incompetence and fraud.

Dirty hospitals kill 5,000 people a year and cost £1 billion. That would pay for policing in Norfolk for nine years.

Clinical negligence claims cost another billion. One in five of all civil servants works for the Ministry of Defence. Yes, we still live in a dangerous world, but do we really need 103,000 civil servants to support 189,000 military personnel?

The Private Finance Initiative has been a bonanza for bankers, consultants and lawyers. The professional fees for Mr Brown's fancy new Treasury Building, another PFI project, come to over £25 million, more than one fifth of the construction cost. A leading City investment banker recently told me: "I like PFI. It's a good source of income. It's good for the business. But as a taxpayer it really pisses me off."

Extra money will only make a difference if two things happen. First, the people running our public services need to be allowed to get on with it, without constant interference. Second, the people who stump up the cash through their taxes need more choice about where they get their services. Patients should be able to choose which hospital they want to be treated in. Parents should be able to choose which school is best for their children.

People don't want the state telling them what to do but to make the choices themselves. Personal taxes are reaching the limits of acceptability and Gordon has reached for the credit cards. But if we stopped the haemorrhage of cash from our public sector we would have to worry less about extra taxes and borrowing.

Mr Brown is running a ghastly experiment to prove that the Nanny State from 1945 to 1979 would have worked just fine if only he had been running it. It is doomed to failure. But it may cost us a fortune before he admits it.

The author is MP for South Norfolk and a member of the Commons Public Accounts Committee. *Managing Resources to Deliver Better Public Services HC 61 - 1 Session 2003 - 2004 published by the National Audit Office.
