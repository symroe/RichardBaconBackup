NHS LIFTs too costly, warns MP

South Norfolk MP Richard Bacon has expressed concern that a £1 billion scheme intended to provide top-class facilities for GPs and other primary care practitioners may prove too costly to roll out across England.

Mr Bacon was speaking as the Commons public accounts committee published its report into the NHS Local Improvement Finance Trusts (LIFTs).

LIFT is a finance scheme whereby ageing GP surgeries are replaced with first-class facilities which put a range of local healthcare professionals under one roof. However, early signs indicate the scheme will be far more expensive than existing funding arrangements.

For example, the annual average cost for primary care accommodation in Newham primary care trust is £10.20 per patient, whereas under NHS LIFT the cost is £32.20 per patient. Only 8.9 per cent of Newham’s patients are served by LIFT facilities. 

This disparity is found elsewhere.  The annual average cost for primary care accommodation in the Ashton, Leigh and Wigan area is £6.90 per patient, which is less than a quarter of the expenditure under NHS LIFT of £29.23 per patient.  In Ashton, Leigh and Wigan, 10.7 per cent of patients are served by NHS LIFT facilities.

There is an even greater discrepancy in the Sandwell area in the West Midlands, where the annual average cost for primary care accommodation is £3.84, compared with £31.41 per patient for NHS LIFT facilities. Around 6.3 percent of Sandwell’s patients are served by LIFT facilities.

Mr Bacon, a member of the committee, said: “That the LIFT scheme provides high-quality facilities for patients is not in doubt.  What is in doubt is whether this level of spending is sustainable. 

“It is likely that much better value for money would be achieved by giving greater control of how funds are spent to local GPs, for example through a standard cost-rent scheme.

“GPs are more than capable of managing and developing new facilities in a cost-effective way”. 

5 July 2006
