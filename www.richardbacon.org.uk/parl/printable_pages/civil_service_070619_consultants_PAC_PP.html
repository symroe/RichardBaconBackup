MP says use of consultants is 'flawed and hideously expensive'


South Norfolk MP Richard Bacon has described the way that the Government uses consultants as ‘flawed and hideously expensive’, as a report finds that improvements in the way consultants are used could save taxpayers more than £500 million a year.

Mr Bacon, a former consultant who was also Deputy Director of the Management Consultancies Association, which represents over 60 leading consulting firms, said:

“Too often when hiring consultants the government fails to ask first if its own civil servants could do the job just as well or better, and more cheaply”.

“Then where consultants are brought in the project management is often poor. Departments don’t collect or share information on how consultants perform. And they don’t plan for transferring the skills to their own staff to build internal capabilities”.

“This is a recipe for repeated failure. In such circumstances it is amazing that projects ever go well. It is no surprise that 40% of public sector clients say they have used consultants when it was not necessary”.

“The whole process is flawed from top to bottom as well as being hideously expensive.  It is time the government brought the use of consultants under proper management”.

Mr Bacon, a member of the Commons public accounts committee, was speaking as the committee published its report on central government’s use of consultants.  The report finds that the government does not know how much money is spent on consultants or whether the end result justifies the cost. 

Consultants are often hired before departments have checked whether in-house staff could do the job instead, and the report also finds that getting a better grip on the use of consultants could save more than £500 million a year, but only if departments are more commercially astute in their use of consultants and how they procure them.

The Cabinet Office efficiency unit reported on the use of consultants in 1994 (“The Government’s use of external consultants” Cabinet Office: An Efficiency Unit scrutiny. August 1994. LONDON : HMSO) and many of its recommendations have been repeated in subsequent reports, including two from the National Audit Office in 2002 and 2006.  To date, there has only been limited progress against these recommendations.

Richard Bacon MP was Deputy Director of the Management Consultancies Association, which represents many of the leading UK consulting firms, from May 1994 until October 1996 after which he worked as a consultant for 3 years.

19 June 2007
