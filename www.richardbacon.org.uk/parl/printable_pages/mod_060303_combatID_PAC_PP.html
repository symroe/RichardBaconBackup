Work faster to stop friendly fire deaths, urges MP


SOUTH NORFOLK MP Richard Bacon has called on the Ministry of Defence to double its efforts in reducing the risk of the armed forces mistakenly firing on their own side because of failure to identify them properly – so-called ‘friendly fire’ - as a new report finds that the Government’s progress in improving battlefield identification has been sluggish. 

Mr Bacon, a member of the Commons public accounts committee, was speaking as the National Audit Office (NAO) published its report on Progress in Combat Identification today (Friday 3 March 2006).  The report was prompted by friendly fire deaths during the 2003 Iraq war and finds that over half of the MoD’s Combat Identification equipment projects have been delayed, deferred or cancelled.  60,000 cards were printed before the conflict to raise soldiers’ awareness of Combat Identification, but became lost in the supply chain before they could reach front-line forces. 

Mr Bacon said: “The MoD’s progress on improving Combat Identification has been patchy.  I find it troubling that over half of the MoD’s Combat Identification projects have stalled or been scrapped; particularly at a time when our armed forces in Iraq and Afghanistan are deployed alongside allied nations in areas where there may be large numbers of civilians.  They need to have confidence not just in their own training and equipment, but those of their coalition partners as well.

“The sad truth is that, in terms of Combat Identification, the position of our armed forces has not advanced significantly since the Iraq war.  It does not help when one measure for improving the situation gets swallowed up by the MoD’s asset tracking problems. 

“It will never be possible to totally remove the risk of friendly-fire incidents, but the MoD still needs to work harder with our allies to find viable solutions and fulfil its duty of care to our servicemen and women.  They have the right to expect the MoD to spare no expense to avoid these needless deaths in future”.

3 March 2006
