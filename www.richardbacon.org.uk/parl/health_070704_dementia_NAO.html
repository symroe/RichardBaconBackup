The NHS must overhaul dementia care, says MP


South Norfolk MP Richard Bacon has called on the NHS to overhaul the way it diagnoses and treats dementia, as a new report finds that too few people are being diagnosed and that early interventions known to be cost-effective are not made widely available.

Mr Bacon, a member of the Commons public accounts committee, was speaking as the National Audit Office (NAO) published its report into improving services and support for dementia sufferers.

The report finds that dementia has not received the priority status from the Department of Health, the NHS or social care that it deserves.

Mr Bacon said: “Britain’s population is ageing, meaning that over the next few years an increasing number of families will be affected by dementia.  However, health and social services give dementia too low a priority and the NHS is poorly equipped to diagnose and treat dementia cases”.  

“Early diagnosis of dementia is known to be cost-effective, but only a third of sufferers ever receive a diagnosis.   Unless the NHS overhauls the way it tackles dementia, then the country will continue to face patchy and costly provision in this area”. 

Dementia costs the economy £14.3 billion a year, including direct costs to the NHS and social care of £3.3 billion a year.  At least 560,000 people in England have dementia and, because of an ageing population, the number of cases is predicted to rise by over 30 per cent over the next 15 years. 

However, dementia has suffered from poor awareness and understanding and the report finds there is a widely held perception that little can be done, leading to a lack of urgency in diagnosing and treating the condition.  Early diagnosis and intervention in cases of dementia is known to be cost-effective, yet only a third to a half of people with dementia ever receive a formal diagnosis.

4 July 2007
