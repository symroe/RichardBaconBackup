Better data on drop-outs needed, says MP


South Norfolk MP Richard Bacon has called on the government to get better data on why students choose to drop out of their courses, as a new report finds that an £800 million investment has failed to cut the drop-out rate.

Mr Bacon said: “Despite a government investment of £800 million over the last five years to help universities hang on to students, the drop-out rate has refused to budge.  One in five students is still failing to complete courses and the government has little idea why.

“Personal or financial problems, illness or disappointment with the choice of university may be behind a student’s decision to leave.  The government needs to get more detailed data on what drives students to quit their courses if it is to cut the drop-out rate”.

Mr. Bacon, MP for South Norfolk, was speaking as the Commons public accounts committee published its report on the retention of students on higher education courses.  Over the last five years, universities have received around Â£800 million to help them retain students.  However, over that period the percentage of students not completing their courses remained at 22 per cent.  Over 100,000 students who started first-degree courses in 2004-05 failed to complete their first year.

The little data that is collected on why students withdraw from their courses is often incomplete and inconsistent.  For example, little is known about the extent to which mental or physical illness or domestic circumstances contribute to withdrawal.  The report finds that universities need good quality information on the reasons for leaving. This can help universities to provide additional support for students, for example for those struggling with elements of their course.

19 February 2008
