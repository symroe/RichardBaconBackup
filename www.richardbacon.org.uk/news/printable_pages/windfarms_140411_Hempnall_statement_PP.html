Richard Bacon MP's statement to the Planning Inquiry into the proposed windfarm at Bussey's Loke, Hempnall


Thank you for providing me with this opportunity to speak and for your flexibility on the date and time.

It will be well known to all the parties to this appeal that on 23 October 2013 TCI Renewables Ltd lodged an appeal with the Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government on the grounds of South Norfolk Council’s failure to give notice of its decision within the appropriate period. 

It will also be well known that, on Wednesday 29 January 2014 members of the Development Management Committee of South Norfolk Council voted 10-1 to determine that the council would have refused planning permission for this application had they been able to determine the application in the normal manner absent TCI Renewables’ appeal to the Secretary of State. 

As all parties to this appeal will be aware of these developments, I do not wish to go into great detail on the chain of events leading up to this inquiry.  Rather, I want to emphasise:

  • First, the need to heed the clearly expressed views of the people of Hempnall;

  • Second, why planning decisions taken locally should not automatically be overridden by national policy objectives at appeal; and

  • Third, how the Secretary of State’s decision to recover this appeal demonstrates clearly the weight he gives to the views of local people.

The people of Hempnall have had this matter hanging over them for almost six years, starting with Enertrag UK Ltd’s initial application for a seven-turbine windfarm in April 2008 through to the new site owners TCI Renewables’ appeal on the grounds of non-determination in October 2013.  I have great admiration for the resilience shown by the people of Hempnall in ensuring that their voice is heard.

Following TCI Renewables’ application for planning permission for four turbines at Hempnall in January 2013, Hempnall residents have gone to extraordinary lengths to make their views clear and unambiguous.  On 18 March 2013, a Parish Poll conducted by South Norfolk Council at the request of Hempnall Parish Council asked residents:

“Do you support the planning application (SNC Ref 2013/0105) by Streetwood Wind Farm Norfolk Limited (Agent’s name: TCI Renewables Limited) for the erection of 4 wind turbines in Hempnall?” [1]


The residents of Hempnall rejected TCI Renewables’ proposal by a margin of four to one.  Half of the residents voted in the poll and 80 per cent of those taking part voted ‘No’.    

In September 2013, TCI Renewables amended its application by removing turbine T2.  Given the change in the application, Hempnall Parish Council decided to consult residents once again.  On 21 October 2013, South Norfolk Council conducted a second Parish Poll which asked:

“Do you support the planning application (SNC Ref 2013/0105 as amended) by Streetwood Wind Farm, Norfolk Limited (Agents name: TCI Renewables Limited) for the erection of 3 wind turbines in Hempnall?” [2]


The residents of Hempnall again voted emphatically to reject TCI Renewables’ amended proposal, this time by 82 per cent. 

It may be argued that the expression of local views contained in these polls does not represent a planning ground upon which this appeal should be dismissed.  However, such grounds absolutely do exist and in my 1 October 2013 submission to South Norfolk Council, I laid out my concerns that these proposals would be in breach of directives contained within paragraphs 98, 118 and 133 of the National Planning Policy Framework; Policy 1 of the Greater Norwich Development Partnership’s Joint Core Strategy; and South Norfolk Council planning policies ENV8 and UTL13.  

It is deleterious for the health of our democracy and the concept of localism if planning decisions taken locally are subsequently overridden by national policy objectives at appeal.  The whole purpose of the Localism Act is to:

“…devolve more powers to councils and neighbourhoods and give local communities greater control over local decisions like housing and planning.” [3]


Therefore, to reject the unambiguous and strengthening views of Hempnall residents expressed twice over at the ballot box would be to misread the clear intent of this Government to place greater emphasis on local decision making on planning matters.  It would also place insufficient weight on the importance of listening to local views.  

On 27 March 2013, the Communities Secretary Eric Pickles announced the final abolition of England’s Regional Spatial Strategies, saying that:

“This government is committed to localism and greater local decision-making in planning. The flawed top-down targets of regional planning, centrally imposing development upon communities, built nothing but resentment.” [4]


Mr Pickles understands the bitterness that can be caused when local views are overridden by national targets and policies at appeal, a point he made again in a Written Ministerial Statement on 6 June 2013, saying that:

“…planning works best when communities themselves have the opportunity to influence the decisions that affect their lives.  However, current planning decisions on onshore wind are not always reflecting a locally led planning system.” [5]

Mr Pickles’ statement went on to promise new planning guidance to assist local councils and planning inspectors in their consideration of individual planning applications, which would set out clearly that:

“…the need for renewable energy does not automatically override environmental protections and the planning concerns of local communities” [6]

That planning practice guidance was published by the Department for Communities and Local Government in July 2013, and states that:

“… all communities have a responsibility to help increase the use and supply of green energy, but this does not mean that the need for renewable energy automatically overrides environmental protections and the planning concerns of local communities.  As with other types of development, it is important that the planning concerns of local communities are properly heard in matters that directly affect them.”  

And that:

“The views of local communities likely to be affected should be listened to.” [7]

Since the publication of this guidance, the Department for Communities and Local Government has continued to insist upon the importance of local views.   Responding to a debate on Planning Policy and Wind Turbines on 12 March 2014, DCLG minister Kris Hopkins told MPs that:

“We are determined to give communities a greater say over the proposals that affect them.” [8]

You will be aware of Mr Hopkins’ subsequent letter to me dated 14 March 2014 in which he confirms that this appeal will be recovered by the Secretary of State for his own determination. 

The Minister also makes it clear he understands the strength and depth of local feeling, citing a total of 1,185 letters of objection to both iterations of these proposals as evidence of local opposition.  Mr Hopkins makes it abundantly clear that this strength of local feeling means, inter alia, that this appeal warrants recovery.   This is surely proof enough of the Government’s determination to ensure that local voices are heard in planning matters. 

In conclusion, the direction of travel of the Government’s policy indicates clearly that the planning system is intended to empower communities, through democratically elected councillors, to decide for themselves how their area should develop.

Whilst national policies and guidance have a place, this government does not believe that such policies should always trump other considerations and it has therefore been staunch in redressing the balance in favour of local views.  I am in total agreement with this view.

The vast majority of the residents of Hempnall have indicated very clearly indeed that they do not want this wind farm and I have laid out in detail the Government’s view that local people should be listened to.  I therefore respectfully ask that you recommend to the Secretary of State that this appeal be dismissed.  

Thank you very much.

RICHARD BACON M.P.

10 April 2014
