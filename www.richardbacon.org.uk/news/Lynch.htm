   MP takes up family's 20-year fight for answers  

The Royal Brompton hospital

A Norfolk couple, whose baby daughter was left severely brain damaged after the failure of both the mains power and the backup generator at a major London hospital, will have their 20-year fight for answers taken up by South Norfolk MP Richard Bacon in the House of Commons.


Sarah Lynch was barely two months old when she underwent
life-saving surgery for severe heart failure in October 1983 at the Royal Brompton Hospital, London. As Sarah recovered from the operation, the Intensive Care Baby Unit was hit by a critical power failure. The backup generator, which was supposed to start immediately, failed to work because of a faulty solenoid, leaving nurses to care for Sarah in darkness with non-functioning equipment.

It was later diagnosed that Sarah had suffered severe and irreversible brain damage at some point, and would need 24-hour care for the rest of her life. Sarah Lynch died aged 11 on 10 February 1995. Sarah's parents, Mike and Sally Lynch, were not told about the power failure and found out accidentally three years after the operation. There was no inquiry into the power failure, even though this was not the first time the backup generator had failed to start.

The Lynch family also discovered that the generator's maintenance logbook and a host of other relevant documentation had been lost or destroyed. Some of Sarah's reports and x-ray plates were also missing. Doctors at the hospital said the power cut would not have caused the brain damage, but they were unable to offer any other explanation.

An independent fact-finder, Dr Dominic Bell, Consultant in Intensive Care and Anaesthesia at Leeds General Infirmary, was appointed but his investigation was hampered by the Royal Brompton's inability to produce vital information. His final report was highly critical of the Royal Brompton's conduct at every level.

Mr Bacon said today:

“The Lynch family have fought for the last 20 years to have someone from the Royal Brompton hospital explain what happened to Sarah, why it happened and why records were either missing or altered”.

“Instead, the hospital has paid lip service to any attempt to resolve this grievance and has seemed content to wait for the Lynch family to give up and go away. However, they have not given up and have instead fought for over two decades to uncover the truth. I hope that tonight's debate will mark the beginning of the end in their search for answers and justice”.

10 January 2006
